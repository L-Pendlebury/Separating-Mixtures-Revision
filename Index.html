1. States of Matter

Solids · Liquids · Gases ▼

All substances exist in one of three states of matter: solid, liquid, or gas. The state depends on how the particles are arranged and how much energy they have. Understanding states of matter is essential for understanding separation techniques.

Solid
Fixed shape & volume

Particles are tightly packed in a regular arrangement. They vibrate but cannot move freely. Solids cannot flow.

Liquid
Fixed volume, no fixed shape

Particles are close together but can move past each other. Liquids flow and take the shape of their container.

Gas
No fixed shape or volume

Particles are far apart and move quickly in all directions. Gases fill any container they are put in.

Fig 0 — Particle arrangements in solids, liquids and gases. Solid particles are tightly packed and regular; liquid particles are close but disordered; gas particles are far apart and move freely.

Changes of state happen when a substance gains or loses energy (heat). The key changes of state are:

  • Melting — solid → liquid (gaining heat)
  • Freezing — liquid → solid (losing heat)
  • Evaporation / Boiling — liquid → gas (gaining heat)
  • Condensation — gas → liquid (losing heat)
  • Sublimation — solid → gas directly (gaining heat), e.g. iodine
💡 Why states of matter matter for separation Separation techniques rely on differences in the physical properties of states — for example, filtration works because the solid cannot flow through filter paper like the liquid can. Distillation works because one liquid becomes a gas at a lower temperature than the other.
💧

2. Dissolving & Solutions

Soluble / Insoluble ▼

When a solid dissolves in a liquid, its particles spread out evenly through the liquid and appear to vanish. The resulting liquid is called a solution. Not every solid dissolves — a substance that dissolves is soluble; one that does not is insoluble.

Soluble examples
Salt · Sugar

Dissolve in water to form a clear solution.

Insoluble examples
Sand · Chalk

Do not dissolve — separated by filtration.

Key vocabulary
Solute — the substance that dissolves (e.g. salt)
Solvent — the liquid it dissolves in (e.g. water)
Solution — solute + solvent mixed together
Mass is always conserved!
Fig 1 — Top: before dissolving — the solid (orange) particles are clustered together in the liquid. Bottom: after dissolving — the particles are spread evenly throughout the liquid, forming a solution.
💡 Conservation of Mass When a physical change like dissolving takes place, mass is always conserved. Dissolve 5 g of salt in 100 g of water and the solution has a mass of exactly 105 g.

Factors that affect how quickly a solid dissolves:

  • Temperature — warmer water means faster dissolving.
  • Particle size — smaller pieces (more surface area) dissolve faster.
  • Stirring — moves fresh solvent against the solid, speeding it up.
🔬

3. Investigating Rate of Dissolving

Fair testing · Variables ▼

A fair test changes only one variable at a time (the independent variable) and measures its effect (dependent variable), keeping everything else the same (control variables).

Fig 5 — Effect of temperature on rate of dissolving. Only temperature is changed — this makes it a fair test.
💡 Surface area and particle size Crushed sugar dissolves faster than a whole cube of the same mass because it has a larger surface area — more sugar is in contact with the water at once.

4. Filtration

Separating insoluble solids ▼

Filtration is used to separate an insoluble solid from a liquid. The mixture is poured through filter paper folded inside a funnel. The tiny pores in the paper let the liquid through but trap the solid particles.

Fig 2 — Filtration apparatus. The residue (insoluble solid) stays in the filter paper; the filtrate (liquid) drips into the conical flask below.
Residue
Solid left behind

Insoluble particles caught in the filter paper.

Filtrate
Liquid that passes through

Collected in the flask below the funnel.

Decanting is a simpler alternative — carefully pouring the liquid off a settled solid without filter paper. It is less thorough than filtration.

🔥

5. Evaporation

Recovering dissolved solids ▼

Evaporation is used to recover a dissolved solid from a solution. Gentle heating turns the solvent (water) into water vapour which escapes, leaving the solid behind as crystals.

Fig 3 — Evaporation setup. The solution is heated gently; water vapour escapes and solid crystals are left in the basin.
⚠️ Safety — Important Do not heat a salt solution to complete dryness. As nearly all the liquid has gone, the salt can spit and crackle. Remove the heat when a crust of crystals begins to form and allow the last of the liquid to evaporate on its own.
💡 Reversible Change Both dissolving and evaporation are reversible changes — no new substance is formed, and the original substances can be recovered.
⚗️

6. Simple Distillation

Liebig Condenser ▼

Distillation is used to separate a soluble solid from a liquid (like salt from water) when you want to collect the liquid rather than the solid. It is also used to separate two liquids with different boiling points.

Simple distillation uses a Liebig condenser — a glass tube surrounded by a water-cooled jacket. Hot vapour from the flask travels into the condenser, cools down, and condenses back into liquid which is collected in a separate flask.

Fig 7 — Simple distillation apparatus. The mixture is heated; vapour travels through the Liebig condenser where it is cooled by water flowing through the outer jacket, condensing back to liquid in the collecting flask.
What it separates
Soluble solid from liquid

e.g. purifying sea water — the pure water is collected, the salt remains in the flask.

How it works
Boiling → Condensing

Liquid boils to vapour, vapour travels to condenser, cools, drips into collecting flask.

Distillation vs Evaporation: Use evaporation when you want the solid; use distillation when you want the liquid.

💡 Fractional distillation When two liquids have different boiling points, fractional distillation can separate them. This is how crude oil is separated into petrol, diesel, kerosene and other fuels at an oil refinery.
🎨

7. Chromatography

KS3 · Identifying substances ▼

Chromatography is used to separate a mixture of dissolved substances that travel at different speeds through a material. It can be used to identify unknown substances by comparing the positions of separated spots.

In paper chromatography, a spot of the mixture is placed on chromatography paper. A solvent (often water or another liquid) moves up the paper by capillary action, carrying the dissolved substances with it. Substances that dissolve more easily in the solvent travel further up the paper.

Fig 8 — Paper chromatography. The solvent travels up the paper, carrying dissolved substances with it. Each substance travels a different distance depending on how strongly it is attracted to the paper vs. the solvent.
⚠️ Important technique point Always draw the baseline in pencil, never in pen — ink contains dyes that would dissolve in the solvent and ruin the chromatogram. The spots of mixture are also placed above the solvent level so they are not washed off immediately.
🧂

8. Extracting Pure Salt from Rock Salt

Real-world application ▼

Rock salt is a mixture of salt (sodium chloride) and rock (insoluble minerals such as sand and grit). Because one component is soluble and the other is insoluble, we need several steps to extract pure salt.

Step 1
Crush
Use a mortar & pestle to crush the rock salt into small pieces
→
Step 2
Dissolve
Add water — salt dissolves, the rock does not
→
Step 3
Filter
Filter to remove the insoluble rock (residue); collect the salt solution (filtrate)
→
Step 4
Evaporate
Heat the filtrate gently — water evaporates, leaving pure salt crystals
Fig 6 — Four-step process to separate salt from rock salt: crush → dissolve → filter → evaporate.

Why this order matters:

  • You must dissolve first — only then can filtration separate the insoluble rock from the salt solution.
  • Filtration comes second — it removes all insoluble rock particles.
  • Evaporation comes last — it recovers pure salt from the filtrate.
💡 Real-world use This is essentially how road salt (grit) is processed from underground deposits, and how salt has been harvested from the sea for thousands of years.
🔀

9. Choosing the Right Method

Decision making ▼

The method you choose depends on whether the solid is soluble or insoluble in the liquid. Sometimes you need both methods in sequence.

Fig 4 — Flowchart for choosing a separation method. Some mixtures need both methods in sequence.

Example — separating sand and salt from water:

  • Step 1 — Filtration: removes the insoluble sand (residue). The salt solution passes through as the filtrate.
  • Step 2 — Evaporation: heats the filtrate to evaporate the water, leaving salt crystals behind.

Solution
A clear mixture formed when a solute dissolves completely in a solvent.
Solvent
The liquid in which a solute dissolves. Water is the most common solvent.
Solute
The substance that dissolves in the solvent to form a solution.
Soluble / Insoluble
A soluble substance dissolves in a solvent (e.g. salt, sugar). An insoluble substance does not dissolve (e.g. sand, chalk).
Filtration
A separation technique that removes insoluble solids from liquids using filter paper in a funnel. The solid cannot pass through the tiny pores.
Filtrate
The liquid that passes through the filter paper and is collected in the flask below.
Residue
The solid left behind in the filter paper after filtration.
Evaporation
When a liquid is heated and turns to gas (water vapour), leaving any dissolved solid behind as crystals.
Decanting
Carefully pouring liquid off a settled solid — a simpler but less thorough alternative to filtration.
Reversible Change
A change that can be undone — no new substance is formed. Dissolving, evaporation, melting, and boiling are all reversible changes.
Conservation of Mass
When a physical change (e.g. dissolving) takes place, the total mass stays exactly the same before and after.
Particle size
How large the individual pieces of a solid are. Smaller particle size → greater surface area → faster dissolving.
Distillation
A separation technique that separates a soluble solid from a liquid (or two liquids with different boiling points) by boiling the mixture and condensing the vapour back to liquid.
Liebig Condenser
A glass tube surrounded by a water-cooled outer jacket. Hot vapour from the flask enters the inner tube, is cooled by cold water in the jacket, and condenses back to liquid.
Condensation
When a gas or vapour cools and turns back into a liquid. The opposite of evaporation. This is what happens in the condenser during distillation.
Chromatography
A technique used to separate and identify a mixture of dissolved substances. Each substance travels a different distance up the paper depending on its attraction to the paper and the solvent.
